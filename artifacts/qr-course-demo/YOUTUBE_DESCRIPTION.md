## Full description

Most "critical thinking" rewards caution — the more hedged your answer, the safer it feels. Constructive Critical Reasoning (CCR) flips that. Here, hedging is the failure mode. The richest, most-falsifiable, most-committed model the evidence supports earns top credit; the cautious "you can't really conclude anything" dodge earns near-zero; and a bold claim the data actively defeats earns zero too.

This video is a full walkthrough of the live CCR app — a self-paced, single-user web course taught, tutored, drilled, and graded entirely by AI. No math, coding, or technical background required.

You'll see the course in motion: reading a lecture and switching it between Short, Medium, and Long depth; asking the section-scoped AI tutor a question and watching the answer stream back token-by-token; taking a homework in one of three formats and getting inverted partial-credit feedback with a written rationale; and reviewing progress on the analytics dashboard.

One unit, eight sections:
• 1.1 The Fecund Lead
• 1.2 Model Selection by Explanatory Yield
• 1.3 Parsimony as a Live Constraint
• 1.4 Abductive Commitment
• 1.5 From Correlation to Mechanism
• 1.6 The Anomaly Cluster
• 1.7 The Cheap Decisive Test
• 1.8 Calibrated Boldness

What's inside:
• Three-depth lessons — read the Short, Medium, or Long version of any section
• A section-scoped AI tutor that streams answers grounded in the exact paragraph you're reading
• One homework per section in your choice of format — MCQ, Hybrid (multiple choice + short written), or Written — with a single, locking attempt
• Inverted partial-credit grading — credit rises with how much data you bind and how falsifiable a test you commit to, and falls for hedging or for claims the data defeats
• Two-layer AI-authorship detection on every submission — a static text classifier plus a keystroke-pattern detector
• A four-phase diagnostic instrument that tracks reasoning growth across the course
• Live analytics — attempts, accuracy, streak, and per-section mastery

Built for middle schoolers, curious adults wanting a brief but meaningful course, instructors evaluating AI-taught coursework, and researchers studying AI academic integrity.
